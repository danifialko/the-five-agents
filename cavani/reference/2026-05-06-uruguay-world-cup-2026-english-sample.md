# Uruguay at the 2026 World Cup: Bielsa Changed the Rules

Seventy-six years. Two World Cup titles, 1930 and 1950, and nothing since. Not for lack of trying. Something else was missing.

Marcelo Bielsa arrived in 2023 and gave it a name.

## What Bielsa Changed

Bielsa didn't manage Uruguay's national team. He rebuilt it. High press, fast transitions, controlled possession. None of this was what Uruguayan football was known for, and nobody expected it to work.

Then they beat Brazil. Then Argentina. Both of them, on form, without excuses.

That's proof of concept. The question is whether it holds in June.

## The Darwin Problem

Every conversation about Uruguay comes back to the same point: Darwin Núñez. He's the striker Bielsa built the attack around. Fast, aggressive, exactly what the system demands.

In the summer of 2025 he moved to Al-Hilal. Then Benzema arrived, foreign player quotas kicked in, and Núñez was pushed out of the league entirely. Since then he's only been playing in the AFC Champions League: 23 appearances, 1,500 minutes total, seven goals.

Bielsa treats match sharpness as non-negotiable. A player who doesn't play loses his rhythm, his legs, his instincts. If Núñez arrives in June half a step slow, Bielsa will genuinely consider leaving him on the bench.

For a team built around him, that's the real risk.

## What They Do Have

Outside the Núñez question, this is a good squad.

Federico Valverde (Real Madrid, 71 caps) is one of the best midfielders in the world right now. Physical, quick-thinking, dangerous in both directions. He doesn't need to be the star to decide games.

José María Giménez (Atlético Madrid, 97 caps) is heading into his fourth World Cup: 2014, 2018, 2022, and now 2026. That kind of experience doesn't have a replacement, especially not in knockout rounds.

Ronald Araújo (Barcelona) adds aerial dominance and an aggression in defense that's rare at this level.

Matías Olivera (Napoli) at left back is a threat going forward, not just a defensive option.

## Group H

Spain, Cape Verde, Saudi Arabia.

Spain is the serious problem. Deep, possession-based, unforgiving. Cape Verde are better than they look on paper. And Saudi Arabia already proved they can beat a favourite, with Argentina in 2022.

The schedule sets Uruguay up well: Saudi Arabia on June 16 in Miami, Cape Verde on June 22, then Spain on June 27 in Guadalajara. Win the first two and they arrive at the Spain game with breathing room.

## The Real Chances

Nobody is picking Uruguay before this tournament starts.

But the knockout record speaks for itself: semi-finals in 2010, quarter-finals in 2018. They always go further than expected.

If Bielsa's system holds, if Núñez arrives fit, and if Giménez and Araújo keep the back line clean, Uruguay can go deep. Not certain. Not even likely. But not as far off as it looks.

Seventy-six years is a long time. It's not forever.
